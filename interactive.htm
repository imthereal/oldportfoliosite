Advanced Orders

Content Strategy at Robinhood
Project Overview
We redesigned the buying and selling order flows for every order type, and also added trailing stops to the mix.
My Contributions
I led Content Strategy for this project. On top of crafting interface content and deliberating over microcopy between the flow variations, I spent time thinking about opportunities to educate users about order types.
We wanted to be able to convey the differernce between each order type within two lines of a mobile interface. Additionally, we wanted to prevent users from using order types they didn't understand. With these goals in mind, I felt that we could best convey tricky "advanced order types" by breaking them down into different components, rather than explaining the mechanics of the order as a whole. This not only reduced the complexity of the explanations within the small space allotted for content, but also helped gate certain order types to users who understood the difficult underlying concepts for each order type.

Take a "stop limit order," for example. We could explain that a stop limit order would be sent to the market at once price (the stop price), then executed at another price (the limit price). But with the component approach, we'd simply say that a limit order is triggered once the stop price is reached. The efficacy of this explanation relies on the user's prior knowledge of a limit order and a stop price—but for our purposes, that was ideal.

We also hypothesized that visuals would be important to understanding order type mechanics, so I worked closely with product design to craft visual representations of each order type.
Rationale
We ran multiple prototype studies and found that the visualizations greatly aided education for advanced order types. We also determined that the component approach to content was effective at both explaining concepts to beginner users, and also gating more complex order types to more advanced users. Interestingly, mid-to-advanced users who understood certain advanced concepts (say, a limit order) were able to extrapolate their knowledge to understand other advanced order types, based on the copy and visuals provided.

We ran in-situ surveys after launching our redesign and received a 4.5/5 for "ease-of-use." Additionally, survey participants frequently cited that the concepts were “explained very well” in the free response portions.
